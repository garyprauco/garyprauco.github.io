We want to discover what kinds of people are in the market for buying bikes, which this dataset provides us in the â€˜Purchased Bikeâ€™ field. We can start by making a few graphs to get a feel for any trends that immediately stick out. Since our data is categorical, it might make more sense to look at some of these trends in terms of percentage of the group, instead of a standard bar chart.

We can see that for the most part, income doesnt have much of an effect on Bike Purchases, there seems to be less overall interest from roughly $45,000 and below, but otherwise bike purchasing habits seem unchanged across all income levels.

Age appears to have a more marked effect, with people roughly between 25-35 buying much more bikes than not, and then a fast taper downward out til 60.

Region shows us proportionally that North America buys less bikes than other regions, and that the Pacific region buys more bikes proportionally. Europe is just about 50/50 on bicycle purchases.

Education seems to show us that as a person recevies more education, they are more likely to purchase a bicylce, with each level increase in education causing a higher proportion of bikes to be purchased.

With commute distance, we see an inverse effect to what we saw with Education. As distance to work increases, bike purchasing likelihood falls with it.

Now that we have a good understanding of what we might expect are important bicycle purchasing factors, lets try and use some more advanced methods to test our hypothesis!

We want to determine what variables affect a persons decision to purchase a bicycle. Since our response variable is a simple Yes/No outcome, it makes sense to use logistic regression! We identified: * Age * Region * Education * Commute Distance As having some type of a trend so lets start with these.

BikeReg <- glm(Purchased.Bike~Age+Region+Education+Commute.Distance,
               family='binomial',
               data=BikeBuyers)

We should take our coefficients and convert them to a more readable format, since our regression table shows our coefficients as log odds, instead of odds, which we can easily convert.
